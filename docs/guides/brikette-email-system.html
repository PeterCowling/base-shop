All three paths end at a Gmail draft that waits for you. The AI never sends on its own.

The one rule that never changes

No email is ever sent automatically. Every draft lands in Brikette / Drafts / Ready for Review in your Gmail. You decide when ‚Äî and whether ‚Äî to send it.

Your daily routine

The inbox is typically triaged twice a day ‚Äî around 09:00 and 17:00 Italy time. Here is what that looks like end-to-end.

  1. Run the inbox triage

    Open Claude Code and run /ops-inbox. The system scans Gmail for new guest emails that haven't been processed yet and shows you a summary list.

  2. Choose which emails to handle

    You'll see a table of waiting emails ‚Äî sender, subject, how long they've been waiting. Say "process all" or pick specific ones.

  3. Watch the AI work (it's quick)

    For each email you selected, the AI reads the full conversation history, looks up FAQs, room details, and policies, then writes a draft reply. It checks its own work before finishing.

    Complaints and difficult situations are flagged ‚Äî not drafted. If an email looks like a serious complaint, refund dispute, or legal threat, the AI skips writing a draft and moves it to a separate folder for you to handle personally. It won't attempt a reply in these cases.

  4. Open Gmail and go to your drafts

    In Gmail, look under Brikette ‚Üí Drafts ‚Üí Ready for Review. Every draft from this triage run is there, already threaded to the correct conversation.

  5. Read, edit if needed, and send

    Read each draft as you would any email you'd written yourself. If everything looks right, press Send. If you want to change something, edit first ‚Äî it's a normal Gmail draft. See Reviewing drafts below for what to check.

Reviewing and sending drafts

You are the last pair of eyes before a draft goes to a guest. A few things worth checking:

  • Does it answer what the guest actually asked? Re-read their email, then check the draft covers each point. The AI is generally thorough, but it can occasionally miss a secondary question buried in a longer email.
  • Is the tone right for this guest? If they were upset, does the reply acknowledge that? If they were friendly, does the reply feel warm?
  • Are specific details correct? Room names, dates, prices, check-in times. The AI draws from our FAQs and policies, but double-check anything time-sensitive or guest-specific.
  • Does the sign-off look right? The draft should close with the usual Brikette sign-off.

The draft is already threaded to the guest's email. When you press Send, it goes as a reply in the same conversation ‚Äî the guest sees it as a normal reply to their email, not a separate message.

Editing before you send

Click into the draft in Gmail and type. It is a normal draft ‚Äî you can change anything. The AI's version is a starting point, not a constraint.

If a draft is clearly wrong

Delete it and write your own reply. Or move the email to Brikette / Queue / Deferred to track it as needing manual attention. Don't leave a bad draft sitting in Ready for Review ‚Äî it won't send itself, but it is easy to accidentally send later.

Watch for the "follow-up needed" line. If the AI couldn't find a confident answer to one of the guest's questions, it inserts a sentence like: "For this specific question we want to give you the most accurate answer ‚Äî Pete or Cristiana will follow up with you directly." When you see this, make sure someone actually does follow up on that point.

Your Gmail labels ‚Äî what they mean

All Brikette emails are organised under the Brikette/ label in Gmail. These labels show you exactly where each email is in the workflow. You don't apply them manually ‚Äî the system does ‚Äî but knowing what they mean helps you navigate quickly.

Labels are only visible to your team ‚Äî guests never see them. When you send a reply, the guest receives a normal email with nothing to indicate how it was organised or processed on your side.

When you need to step in

T&C confirmations ‚Äî Needs-Decision

Sometimes a guest replies in a way that looks like they're accepting the terms and conditions, but the phrasing isn't clear enough for the system to be certain. Rather than guess, it pauses and puts the email in Queue / Needs-Decision.

What to do: Open the email and read the guest's reply. If they've clearly agreed ‚Äî even informally, like "Yes, that's fine" or "OK, go ahead" ‚Äî confirm it in the triage tool. The system will then continue with the payment workflow. If you're genuinely unsure, treat it as not confirmed and follow up with the guest directly asking them to clarify.

Escalated emails ‚Äî Deferred

The AI will not draft a reply for:

  • Serious complaints or repeated grievances
  • Refund disputes or chargeback threats
  • Legal language or fraud accusations
  • High-value disputes (generally over ‚Ç¨500)

These go to Queue / Deferred with no draft created. You write the reply. Take time with these ‚Äî they need a personal, considered response, not a quick template.

Tip: Reply directly from within the Gmail thread so the conversation stays together. After you send, you can move the email to Outcome / Drafted manually to keep the label history tidy.

Booking payment emails

When a new booking notification arrives, the system automatically prepares a payment request email to send to the guest. If payment isn't received, it follows up automatically ‚Äî always placing a draft in your Gmail for you to send.

No payment ‚Äî 2nd request Second payment request drafted for you to review and send. Workflow / Prepayment-Chase-2

Do not edit the payment terms or cancellation conditions. These emails have fixed, legally-reviewed wording. If you'd like to add a personal note, add it after the standard text ‚Äî not within it.

T&C agreement during the payment process

Guests often reply to the payment email with something like "Yes, I accept" alongside their payment details. The system detects this automatically. If the wording is ambiguous, it moves to Needs-Decision for you to confirm before payment proceeds.

Cancellations

When a cancellation notification arrives from the property management system, it's handled separately. The system:

  • Reads the cancellation details (guest name, dates, booking reference)
  • Prepares a cancellation acknowledgement draft for you to review and send
  • Labels the email Workflow / Cancellation-Received (and Cancellation-Processed once complete)

If the cancellation notice is in an unexpected format, it's labelled Cancellation-Parse-Failed and you'll need to handle it manually.

How the AI writes replies

The AI doesn't write emails from scratch ‚Äî it works from a library of pre-approved templates (over 50, covering the most common guest scenarios). When an email arrives, it picks the best matching template, then personalises it with the guest's name, dates, and other details from the inquiry.

To personalise accurately, it draws on:

  • Our FAQ ‚Äî frequently asked questions and their approved answers
  • Room details ‚Äî configurations, capacity, amenities
  • Policies ‚Äî check-in rules, age restrictions, house rules
  • Bar & breakfast prices ‚Äî current menu pricing
  • Writing style guide ‚Äî so replies sound like Brikette, not a generic bot
  • The conversation history ‚Äî so it doesn't repeat things already said or contradict earlier promises

Personalised greetings and in-line knowledge

Templates now use slots ‚Äî named placeholders that are filled at send time. The greeting slot is filled with the guest's name when known ("Dear Maria," rather than "Dear Guest,"). A knowledge-injection slot lets the AI add relevant FAQ snippets or policy details at exactly the right point in the email ‚Äî not tacked on at the end.

Because replies are template-based, the core content ‚Äî pricing, policies, house rules ‚Äî is always consistent. The personalisation on top is what makes them feel natural.

If no template fits, or the AI can't fill in a detail confidently, it says so. Rather than guess, it inserts: "For this specific question we want to give you the most accurate answer ‚Äî Pete or Cristiana will follow up with you directly." When you see this line in a draft, make sure someone follows up on that point.

How the system gets smarter over time

The email system doesn't stay static. Every triage session quietly collects signals about which templates worked well and which needed rewriting. Over time, this helps the AI pick better templates ‚Äî and flag ones that need updating. You don't need to do anything to make this work. It runs in the background.

Signal capture ‚Äî what's recorded

At the end of each triage session, the system knows which template was used for each email, and whether the AI rewrote it significantly before creating the draft. A template that needs a big rewrite every time it's used is a sign it's out of date or being selected for the wrong type of question.

These signals are stored privately and are never visible to guests. They include no personally identifiable information ‚Äî just which template category was used and how much editing was required.

Template proposals ‚Äî human approval always required

When the system notices a pattern ‚Äî for example, the "transportation" template was heavily rewritten 8 times in a row ‚Äî it generates an improvement proposal. This proposal sits in a queue until Pete reviews it.

Human approval gate

No template is ever changed without Pete reviewing and approving the change. Proposals describe what changed and why. Pete can approve, reject, or ignore them. If a proposal isn't reviewed within 30 days, it expires automatically.

Ranker calibration ‚Äî better template matching

Separately from proposals, the system periodically recalibrates which templates it prefers for which types of question. Templates that consistently work well (minimal rewriting needed) get a small ranking boost. Templates that consistently require heavy rewrites get a small ranking penalty.

The adjustment is intentionally gentle ‚Äî the ranking never swings more than 30 points in either direction, and it takes at least 20 real sessions to trigger a recalibration. This prevents the system from over-learning from a handful of unusual emails.

Session summary ‚Äî signal health

At the end of each triage session (when the queue is empty), the summary now includes a signal health block alongside the usual processed counts:

  • How many selection and refinement signals were captured this session
  • How many template improvement proposals are currently pending review
  • A reminder to run the ranker calibration when enough signals have accumulated

If the summary shows >10 pending proposals, that's a backlog warning. It's worth spending a few minutes reviewing and approving or rejecting them ‚Äî stale proposals expire after 30 days so they won't pile up indefinitely, but frequent backlogs suggest templates need more attention.

If something seems wrong

An email is stuck with no draft appearing

Occasionally a crash or interruption can leave an email in a processing state with no draft appearing. The system detects and fixes this automatically at the start of the next triage run ‚Äî usually within 30 minutes. If it has been stuck for more than an hour, flag it to Pete.

A draft looks completely wrong

Delete it and write your own reply. Note the subject line and date so the FAQ or knowledge base can be checked and updated if the AI was working from outdated information.

An email keeps appearing with an Error label

The next triage run retries automatically. If the same email keeps showing up as an Error, flag it to Pete ‚Äî there may be something unusual about that email's format.
